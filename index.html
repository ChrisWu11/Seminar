English Summary

This paper dives into how putting students from different cultures together in group work at UK universities might help them build intercultural competence, things like being more open-minded, empathetic, and flexible when dealing with people from other backgrounds. It's a longitudinal mixed-methods study at one British uni, focusing on postgrad students in Business, Education, and Engineering. They used the Multicultural Personality Questionnaire (MPQ) to track changes in IC over 8 months with pre- and post-surveys on 370 students, plus three rounds of interviews with 14 students and some staff. Staff and students generally see mixed groups as a good way to boost cultural awareness and learn from diverse views, but students found it rewarding yet super challenging—stuff like language barriers, cultural misunderstandings, and feeling excluded or frustrated, especially in high-stakes assessed work. Key finding: international students' open-mindedness actually dropped over time, particularly in Engineering where home students outnumbered internationals and tensions were high, while flexibility improved a bit in Business. Without proper teacher guidance or intentional setup (like explaining the purpose or mediating conflicts), these groups can backfire and make people less tolerant long-term. The authors suggest better staff training, reflective activities, and embedding IC assessment to make it work, rather than just throwing people together.

English Summary

This article explores what life is like for international undergrads at UK universities, zeroing in on how much true intercultural stuff— like building mutual understanding and tolerance—actually happens in uni life. It's from a two-year ESRC-funded project across four English unis (two 'old' research-focused, two 'new' teaching-oriented), using surveys of 228 students at the start and end, plus in-depth case studies with 11 diverse students over time, including interviews and a focus group. They frame it with Allport's contact theory: for positive intercultural contact, you need equality, common goals, cooperation, and authority support, plus frequent pleasant interactions. Findings show tons of potential—unis are diverse bubbles in a tolerant society, and students grow in confidence, cosmopolitan identity, and self-efficacy over two years, appreciating multicultural vibes and adapting to new pedagogy. But limits abound: students often stick to similar cultural groups for comfort, face financial/language barriers feeling unequal early on, compete academically without shared purposes, and get patchy support from tutors (some great, some not). Outside uni contact is rare due to the campus bubble. Overall, while transformative internationalization is possible, superficial stuff like fee-chasing dominates, and students self-manage a lot, but unis could do more to nurture real intercultural growth.

English Summary

This mixed-methods pilot study checks how social media affects undergrads (mostly education majors) during a 4-week study abroad in Europe, specifically if they feel that "Fear of Missing Out" (FoMO)—anxiety about missing home events or friends' fun—kicks in. They surveyed 22 US students with scales on FoMO and social media intensity, plus focus groups with 19. Based on Allport's ideas indirectly, it looks at social capital (bonding/bridging ties) and self-presentation. Findings: students used platforms like Facebook, Instagram, Twitter mainly for purposeful stuff—posting pics to promote their adventures (bragging to make others jealous), coordinating with fellow travelers via GroupMe/Facebook groups for trips/homework, and staying connected to family/friends back home to fight homesickness without mindless scrolling. FoMO was low overall (mean 2.2/5); most didn't miss home much due to the short trip and exciting abroad life, but a couple felt disconnected/anxious early on. Interestingly, they flipped FoMO—trying to induce it in others by timing posts for max likes and visibility, boosting their social capital. No big gender/age diffs, but unreliable WiFi limited use. Authors suggest faculty harness social media for programs (e.g., promote via student posts) but guide it to avoid distractions, especially for language immersion, and note longer trips might amp up FoMO.
